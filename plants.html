The Plant Collection

Ten frost-free acres. Rare palms. Exotic fruit trees. Native Florida species. A butterfly garden. Three lakes. And a scientific record that grows every time a visitor pulls out their phone and takes a photo.

Browse the collection → View on iNaturalist

"We are still learning what lives here. That's not a gap in our knowledge — it's an invitation."

The park began in 1990 as a county palm nursery. The rare palm collection reflects that origin — specimens you simply won't find in cultivation anywhere else in Manatee County thrive here because of the park's uniquely frost-free microclimate.

In 2008, the Manatee Rare Fruit Council established the Exotic Fruit Trail: star fruit, dragon fruit, jackfruit, lychee, sapodilla, tamarind, guava, persimmon, pomelo, and over two dozen other species on a single walk. Most visitors walk right past it.

The butterfly garden uses native Florida host plants — designed not just to attract butterflies, but to sustain them through their full life cycle. Come in the morning. Bring a camera. Give it ten minutes of quiet and you'll understand why this place matters.

Our iNaturalist project is where the park's scientific record lives. Every observation — plant, bird, insect, fungus — adds to what we know about this ten acres. The data is public, permanent, and connected to the global biodiversity record.

Featured plants

A handful of what makes this place worth visiting. The full directory has hundreds more.

🌴
Bismarck Palm
Bismarckia nobilis
Rare Exotic

One of the most striking palms in the collection. The silver-blue fronds are unlike anything else in the park. Native to Madagascar.
🍈
Jackfruit
Artocarpus heterophyllus
Fruit Trail Exotic

The largest tree-borne fruit in the world. The specimens on the Exotic Fruit Trail are productive and impressive. Many visitors have never seen one.
🦋
Firebush
Hamelia patens
Native

A Florida native that's one of the best butterfly magnets in the Southeast. Blooms nearly year-round. Core plant of the butterfly garden.
🌺
Royal Poinciana
Delonix regia
Exotic

In full bloom in late spring, it stops people in their tracks. The park's specimen is mature and spectacular. Hard to photograph badly.
🌿
Beach Sunflower
Helianthus debilis
Native

Salt-tolerant native groundcover, planted along the park's pond margins. Blooms constantly. Beloved by native bees and the volunteers who planted it.
🍋
Lychee
Litchi chinensis
Fruit Trail

Not easy to grow in Florida without a frost-free microclimate. The park has it. Come in June when the fruit is ripe.

Your observation matters.

Every photo you take in the park — of a plant, a bird, a butterfly, a fungus — can become a permanent part of the park's scientific record. iNaturalist makes that possible with your phone.

The live counter at the top of this page updates in real time. When you log an observation, you'll see it there. The park's volunteers notice. Bev notices. It adds up.

Join the iNaturalist project →

How to add an observation

  1. Download the iNaturalist app — free on iOS and Android
  2. Walk through the park and photograph anything interesting. Plants, insects, birds, fungi — anything.
  3. Open the app and submit your photo. AI and community volunteers will help identify it.
  4. Once two experts agree on an ID, it becomes Research Grade — part of the global biodiversity record.
  5. Your observation shows up on the park's project page and in the live counter on this site.

You don't need to know what something is to photograph it. That's what the community is for.
